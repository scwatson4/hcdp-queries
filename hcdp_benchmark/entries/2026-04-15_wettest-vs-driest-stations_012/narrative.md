## Scientific context

Hawaii has some of the steepest rainfall gradients on Earth, driven almost entirely by orographic effects. Persistent northeast trade winds carry warm, moist Pacific air toward the islands. When that air encounters volcanic terrain, it is forced upward, cools adiabatically, and releases moisture as heavy precipitation on windward slopes. The leeward coasts, sitting in the rain shadow, receive a fraction of that rainfall. This pattern produces extreme contrasts over short distances -- wetter than the Amazon on one side of a ridge, drier than Los Angeles on the other.

## The 12.5x wet/dry ratio

The data confirms this pattern in striking fashion. Piihonua, at 606m on the windward Hamakua coast of the Big Island, receives an estimated 4,414mm (174 in) of rainfall per year. Just 75km away on the same island, Kawaihae on the leeward Kohala coast receives only 353mm (14 in) -- a 12.5x difference. This ratio, measured between stations on the same island, illustrates the power of orographic forcing more vividly than any textbook diagram.

## Frequency drives the difference, not intensity

A natural assumption might be that wet stations get heavier rain events. The data shows otherwise. What separates wet from dry is how often it rains, not how hard. Wet stations receive measurable rainfall on 68-71% of all days -- essentially a light-to-moderate shower is the default condition. Dry stations see rain on only 9-14% of days. This is because the trade wind orographic mechanism produces frequent, moderate precipitation on windward slopes, not occasional deluges.

Crucially, per-event intensity does not follow the same pattern. Even the driest stations can experience extreme single-day rainfall when rare storm systems (such as upper-level lows, kona storms, or tropical cyclone remnants) override the normal trade wind regime. Lipoa, the second-driest station at only 494mm/yr, once recorded 203mm in a single day -- more than 40% of its typical annual total. This has important implications for flood risk: dry areas are not safe from extreme precipitation.

## Geographic pattern

The geographic sorting is clean:

- **All 5 wettest stations** are windward: three on the Big Island (Piihonua, Waipoo, Spencer -- all on the northeastern Hamakua/Hilo coast) and two on Oahu (Lyon Arboretum and Kalawahine, both in the windward Manoa Valley / Tantalus area).
- **All 5 driest stations** are leeward or rain-shadow: three on Maui (Lipoa, Kula, Olowalu -- leeward west Maui and upcountry Haleakala rain shadow) and two on the Big Island (Kawaihae and Palamanui -- leeward Kohala/Kona coast).

No station breaks the windward-wet / leeward-dry rule, and no island is uniformly wet or dry. The Big Island appears in both lists, illustrating that the gradient is controlled by aspect relative to trade winds, not by island identity.

## Station 0115 (Piiholo) exclusion

Station 0115 at Piiholo, Maui would appear as the wettest station by far if included, with an apparent annual rainfall of approximately 616,588mm/yr. This is obviously erroneous. Investigation revealed that the station's rainfall sensor periodically reports values of 7999mm, which are error codes rather than real measurements. The total error contribution is approximately 1,999,750mm compared to only 5,427mm of plausible real rainfall. Including this station would produce a misleading ranking, so it is excluded from the analysis. This is a common pitfall in automated climate data analysis -- sensor error codes that look like extreme values can dramatically distort summary statistics if not recognized and filtered.
