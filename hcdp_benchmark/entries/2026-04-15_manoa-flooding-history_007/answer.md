The most recent Manoa Valley flooding was most likely **March 23, 2026**, not March 13 as initially assessed. While March 13 had higher total daily rainfall at Lyon Arboretum (198mm vs 136mm), March 23 was far more dangerous for urban flooding:

- **Peak hourly intensity of 50.8 mm/hr** (2 inches/hour) — triple the March 13 peak of 16.8 mm/hr. This overwhelms storm drain capacity instantly.
- **Saturated soil at 63% VWC** from 148mm of rain on March 20 (3 days prior), plus continued rain on March 21-22. The ground had zero absorption capacity.
- **355mm cumulative rainfall over 4 days** (March 20-23) across the entire watershed.
- **Napuumaia station (508m, ridge above UH Manoa) recorded 163mm** — the heaviest of any Manoa station that day, all of which drains downhill toward campus.
- 7 of 8 Manoa-area stations exceeded 50mm; all upstream stations (Kalawahine 117mm, Upper Waolani 133mm, Nuuanu Reservoir 112mm) were heavily impacted.

March 13, 2026 was also a significant event (197.6mm at Lyon Arboretum, saturated soil) but its more moderate hourly intensity (16.8 mm/hr max) spread the rainfall over many hours, giving drainage systems more time to handle flow.
