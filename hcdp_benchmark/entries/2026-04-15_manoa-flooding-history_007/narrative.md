## Scientific context

Urban flash flooding in Manoa Valley is a recurring hazard driven by the valley's funnel-shaped topography, which concentrates runoff from the Koolau ridgeline into Manoa Stream. Flood risk depends on three factors: (1) rainfall intensity, (2) antecedent soil moisture (saturated soil produces 100% runoff), and (3) upstream watershed conditions (the valley collects runoff from Kalawahine, Waolani, and the upper Koolau slopes).

## Methodology

Analyzed the two highest-rainfall events at Manoa-area stations across four dimensions:
1. **Daily rainfall totals** at 6 Manoa-area stations (Lyon Arboretum, Waolani, Upper Waolani, Napuumaia, Kalawahine, Nuuanu Reservoir)
2. **Multi-day rainfall context** — consecutive-day totals to identify prolonged events
3. **Antecedent soil moisture** (SM_1_Avg at Lyon Arboretum) in the days before each event
4. **Hourly rainfall intensity** — sub-daily resolution to assess flash flood potential

## Comparative analysis

### March 13, 2026 — HIGH flood likelihood
- 197.6mm at Lyon Arboretum, 217.9mm at Kalawahine, 177.8mm at Waolani
- Came after 2 days of heavy precursor rain (64.3 + 52.1mm on Mar 11-12)
- Soil already at 64% VWC before the main event — near saturation, minimal absorption capacity
- Peak soil moisture reached 66.2% on Mar 14 — effectively saturated
- Sustained hourly intensity: 5 hours exceeded 11mm/hr
- All 10 Oahu mesonet stations exceeded 100mm; Kaala summit hit 357mm
- The entire Manoa/Nuuanu watershed was simultaneously drenched

### January 26, 2025 — MODERATE flood likelihood
- 195.3mm at Lyon Arboretum — nearly identical daily total
- But preceded by dry days (soil at 45.7% VWC — significant absorption capacity remaining)
- Single-day burst, not multi-day event; 14.7mm/hr peak concentrated in one hour
- Soil jumped from 46% to 60% — the ground absorbed ~14% VWC worth of water
- Still a significant event, but the drier antecedent conditions substantially reduced runoff

### Other notable events
- October 26, 2024: 155mm at Kalawahine, 135mm at Lyon Arboretum
- December 20, 2023: 117mm at Napuumaia

## Conclusion

The March 13, 2026 event was the most likely recent flood in Manoa ��� not because it had the most raw rainfall (it was nearly tied with January 2025), but because the combination of saturated soil, multi-day precursor rain, and heavy simultaneous upstream precipitation created conditions where virtually all rainfall became surface runoff. This is a classic example of why antecedent soil moisture is critical for flood prediction.

## Limitations

- No stream gauge data available to confirm actual flooding
- Soil moisture is measured at one depth; deeper soil layers may behave differently
- NWS flood warnings and emergency reports would provide ground-truth confirmation
- The mesonet record only starts ~2022, so earlier Manoa floods (e.g., the well-known 2004 event) are not captured
